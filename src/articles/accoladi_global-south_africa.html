Accoladi Global: South Africa

Overview

Though many may think that studying internationally is only for the wealthy, studying in a foreign country may actually be more affordable for the average family than studying in the United States.  One of the most diverse countries in the world, studying in South Africa will expose you to many different cultures, and many South African universities offer quality fine arts programs for a fraction of the price of American schools.  Let’s examine the costs and benefits of studying in South Africa and how to make it a reality.

Visa Requirements

South Africa falls in the middle concerning the strictness of student visa requirements.  Like most countries, you must have health insurance that is valid in South Africa before your visa application can be accepted.  Additionally, you must be accepted (or provisionally accepted) by a South African institution, and, if under 21, you and your parents/guardians must nominate a local guardian in South Africa to sponsor your studies.  You also must show that you are able to financially support yourself while you study in South Africa by having access to 3000R ($215) per month.  This includes bank statements, cash on hand, traveller’s cheques, credit cards, scholarships, etc. You also must pay a $125 visa application fee.  The processing time for a South African study visa is between 4 and 8 weeks.  You can visit visaimmigration.co.za for more information on the process of attaining a study visa to South Africa.

Compared to the United States

In order to see the benefits of studying in South Africa, let’s compare three South African universities to three American universities.  We will compare two small institutions, two medium institutions, and two large institutions on factors such as tuition, total cost, curriculum, and extracurricular and performance opportunities.

Rhodes University vs College of the Holy Cross

First, let’s compare two small institutions: Rhodes University in Makhanda, South Africa and College of the Holy Cross in Worcester, MA, USA.  In terms of population, Rhodes has 5,600 undergraduates, and Holy Cross has over 3,000.  The tuition costs of the two are stark contrasts.  Holy Cross charges $54,740 in tuition alone, and total cost can reach up to $72,000 per year!  However, Rhodes charges only $5,000 in tuition for international students, and the total cost (including housing, travel, personal expenses, food, fees, etc) reaches around $14,000 per year.

Both institutions have small but excellent music programs, and both offer general music degrees with specializations available.  Holy Cross offers a 4 year BA in Music with a specialization in an instrument or voice available.  Rhodes, on the other hand, offers two pathways for a music degree.  The first is the BA in Music, a 3 year program that teaches general music theory, history, and performance, and this degree can be combined with another BA program.  The second route at Rhodes is the BMus (Bachelor in Music), which is a 4 year program, and during a student’s fourth year, he or she selects five papers for the year that outline his or her specific interests in music, from a composition portfolio to a paper on music history.

Both Rhodes and Holy Cross offer similar opportunities in terms of performance.  Both offer audition and non-audition choirs, jazz band, orchestra, and string ensemble.  Rhodes also offers wind band, and Holy Cross, being a Catholic college, offers chapel choir.  Overall, Rhodes University offers similar programs and opportunities to Holy Cross at a much more affordable price.

Stellenbosch University vs University of Southern California

At 19,000 and 20,500 students, respectively, Stellenbosch University, located on the Western Cape, and University of Southern California, located in Los Angeles, CA, have similar undergraduate student body sizes and both fall in the middle, size-wise.  Despite their similar size, they differ greatly in affordability;  Stellenbosch charges around $4,100 in tuition with a total yearly cost of about $13,700, while USC charges around $60,500 in tuition with a total yearly cost of about $81,700.

Both are prestigious universities, and both offer extensive curricula in their schools of music.  Stellenbosch, like Rhodes, offers two degree routes: the BA in Music and the BMus.  The BA in Music is a 3 year program that focuses on practical music study, musicology, music theory, and teaching methods, and students add an elective subject from the humanities, including studies in psychology, mathematics, Xhosa, English, and Afrikaans.  The BMus is a 4 year program; students receive foundational music training their first two years, and they choose a concentration their third year. Students can choose composition, music technology, musicology, music education, conducting, or performance and are able to specialize in an instrument of their choice in addition to their concentration.

University of Southern California also offers a plethora of music programs; however, while USC and Stellenbosch offer similar programs, USC’s programs are more specialized because instead of students receiving the same musical training their first two years, students are trained in a specific field all four years.  Students may earn a BM in choral music, composition, jazz studies, music production, or performance; a BS in music industry; or a BFA in musical theatre.  

Additionally, both have similar ensemble opportunities.  Both have choirs, orchestra, jazz ensembles, and bands, though USC offers slightly more ensemble opportunities.  Overall, both of these prestigious universities offer in-depth music programs, yet Stellenbosch is far more affordable than USC.

North-West University vs Arizona State University

Both North-West University, located in Potchefstroom, South Africa, and Arizona State University, located in Phoenix, AZ, are large public universities with an undergraduate population of 50,000 and 44,000, respectively, and both offer BA and BM/BMus degree paths.  

NWU’s BA in Music and Society is a 3 year program that offers basic musical training and focuses on music education and community music.  On the other hand, NWU’s BMus is a 4 year program, and during the third year, a student chooses a specialization in performance, methodology, musicology, music education, music theory, or composition.  Arizona State also offers both BA and BM programs, and these programs are similar to NWU.  Arizona State offers a BA in Music, a BA in Music and Culture, and a BA in Popular Music, and it offers a BM in Music Education, a BM in Music Therapy, a BM in Performance (with a specialization in instrument), and a BM in Theory and Composition.  They both give students many performance opportunities by offering a plethora of ensembles to students.

These two large universities are very similar in terms of size, curriculum, and performance opportunities, but they differ greatly in cost.  In-state tuition for Arizona State University is $10,710, and out-of-state tuition is $28,800, bringing the estimated total cost to $29,090 and $47,180, respectively.  North-West University charges international students $1,500 in tuition, and the estimated total yearly cost is around $7,200.

Conclusion

As the tables show, a music student can get a similar education to that of the United States in South Africa for considerably less money.  In addition, studying in South Africa will expose you to many different cultures and give you experiences you won’t find in the United States.  Plus, the cost of living is very low in South Africa compared to the United States and other English-speaking countries.  For these reasons, South Africa is a viable option for American students who seek to earn a quality education for a low price.
