Musical Terms and Sightreading

Just as pitch and rhythm are essential to performing a musical score, musical terms are just as critical since they denote the stylistic interpretation of pitch and rhythm. Simply put, it is the knowledge of and the ability to translate musical terms into sound and emotion that ultimately gives the performed score its musical life. For this reason, musical terms are an essential aspect of testing during the audition process for admission and scholarship consideration in music departments and schools of music. How music departments test a potential students’ knowledge of musical terms varies.

The first and most basic testing of musical terms knowledge is defining a listing musical terms either in writing on an entrance exam or verbally to the adjudicators as a part of the performance aspect of the audition. When tested verbally, the audition adjudicator will most often ask the potential student to define musical terms that are denoted in the student’s performed solo and/or etude compositions. Stories abound about a certain piano professor in a school of music in the Northeast who is renowned for taking the adjudicator’s provided score of the auditioner’s solo material and marking out the printed musical terms and replacing the terms of his choosing. He then hands the student his marked-up score with his handwritten terms and requests the student perform the now marked-up solo with these new musical terms.

Research shows that some schools of music list musical terms in a random order and request they be put in matching categories and in a progressive order within those categories. An example from an entrance exam from a school of music in the Midwest: Dynamics, Andante, Tenor, Forte, Pianissimo, Grave, Soprano, Mezzo-Piano, Moderato, Lento, Alto, Tempo Markings, Fortississimo, Allegro, Baritone, Piano, Presto, Voice Parts, Fortissimo, Adagio, Bass, Mezzo-Forte, Pianississimo, and Vivace.

The correct answer would be:

Dynamics Voice Parts Tempo Markings
Pianississimo Soprano Grave
Pianissimo Alto Lento
Piano Tenor Adagio
Mezzo-Piano Baritone Andante
Mezzo-Forte Bass Moderato
Forte Allegro
Fortissimo Vivace
Fortississimo Presto

Most often the adjudicator wants to know if the potential student can apply the musical terms with the correct interpretation within the context of a performance. A West Coast school of music combines the testing of musical terms and sightreading. The potential student is handed three sightreading examples. There are distinct similarities in each of these three examples as all have the same meter, rhythmic patterns, and intervals. Each example is in a different key, and the musical terms are different in each example. In this case, the school is looking for the student’s ability to show how the musical interpretation (and key) delineates one musical example from the others.

As you study and prepare for your audition, remember, pitch and rhythm in a musical score are essential. But the understanding of and the ability to appropriately apply the knowledge of musical terms is just as essential for a musical performer. Without question, musical terms will be a part of the collegiate audition process. Be sure you are prepared.
